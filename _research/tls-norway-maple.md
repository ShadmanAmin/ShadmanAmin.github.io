My honours thesis at Colby College, supervised by Dr. Justin Becknell in the
Forest Ecosystems Lab, applied terrestrial laser scanning to a management
question: how do you quantify the structural effect of invasive Norway maple
(*Acer platanoides*) on a Maine forest without felling any of it?

TLS produces a dense three-dimensional point cloud of the stand, from which
aboveground biomass and composition can be estimated non-destructively. I
processed the scans and associated imagery in RiSCAN and Metashape Pro,
analysed the results in ENVI, R and Python, and looked in particular at edge
effects — how proximity to a forest boundary shapes both ecosystem services and
the diversity of invasive species establishing there.

Alongside the thesis I wrote the lab's TLS field protocol, which as far as I
know is still in use (in UCSB as well!). This was my first real encounter with the idea that
half of a remote sensing project is deciding, in the field, exactly where to
put the instrument.
